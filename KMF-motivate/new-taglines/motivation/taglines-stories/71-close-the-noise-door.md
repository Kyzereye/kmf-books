# Close The Noise Door

*Close The Noise Door*

Noise does not ask permission; you have to close the door. Shut one channel of noise before you begin and watch how much easier the first step becomes.

## Category

**Motivation** — Background noise feels harmless until it eats the attention you need to begin; *Close The Noise Door* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by removing one input channel before the first step so your mind has room to move.

## Theme

**Guard Your Ground** — Closing a noise channel before you start is a boundary move that protects the quiet ground where beginning actually happens.

## The idea

Noise rarely arrives as a crisis. It arrives as a podcast in the background, a television you are not watching, a notification stack, a group chat that never quite goes quiet. Each one seems small. Together they fill the room until starting feels like shouting over a crowd. You do not need perfect silence to begin — but you do need to stop pretending that constant input is neutral. When you close one noise door before you start, you are not being fragile. You are making room for the first step to land somewhere it can be heard.

## Story

Maria worked from a kitchen table while her partner watched game highlights with the volume low enough to call "background." She told herself she was used to it. She also told herself she could not focus before noon, which was roughly when the house emptied out and she finally made progress on her client proposals.

One Tuesday the proposal was due and the house was full. Instead of waiting for quiet, she closed one door: headphones, not music — just silence — and the television off for her hour. Her partner raised an eyebrow. She said she needed one channel closed, not the whole world.

The difference was immediate and almost embarrassing. The same task that had taken three fragmented hours on Monday took fifty minutes. Her fingers moved. Sentences connected. The noise had not been destroying her life. It had been costing her the first clean thought she needed to enter the work.

Maria did not become someone who needs a monastery. She became someone who stopped leaving every door open and wondering why beginning felt so heavy.

## Takeaway

Identify one noise channel you leave open by habit — audio, alerts, a screen you are not using — and close it for the first thirty minutes of your next start. The work may not get easier, but you will finally hear yourself begin.
