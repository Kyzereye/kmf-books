# Start Sore

*Start Sore*

You do not need to feel fresh to begin. Start sore, start tired, start anyway — motion often loosens what waiting never healed.

## Category

**Motivation** — Waiting for the perfect body or mood keeps you on the couch; *Start Sore* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by beginning in the condition you actually have instead of the one you keep waiting for.

## Theme

**Push Through** — Starting when you feel stiff, depleted, or worn down is the move of staying in the fight when comfort says rest one more day.

## The idea

The mind treats soreness and fatigue as a stop sign. You wake up tight, drained from yesterday, or simply not restored, and the reasonable voice says wait until you feel better. But waiting rarely heals what motion addresses. Rest has its place, yet endless postponement turns a stiff shoulder into a month without training, a tired morning into a week without writing. When you start sore, you stop negotiating with a body that may never feel fresh on command. Movement often loosens what stillness prolongs — not because pain is noble, but because the first steps teach you whether you are actually broken or merely reluctant.

## Story

Derek skipped three runs in a row because his calves ached from the weekend hike and his sleep had been thin. Each morning he told himself he would go when he felt recovered. By Thursday the ache had settled into a dull stiffness that felt permanent, and the habit felt farther away than it had on Monday.

Friday he laced up anyway. The first block was exactly as bad as he feared — heavy legs, shallow breath, the urge to walk home and call it a recovery day. He did not chase speed. He jogged slow enough to keep moving and honest enough to admit he was not injured, just out of rhythm.

By mile two his stride opened. Not fresh — never fresh — but functional. The soreness did not vanish. It changed from a reason to stop into background noise he could work through.

That afternoon he opened the draft he had been avoiding for the same reason: not enough energy, not the right headspace. He wrote for twenty minutes sore and tired and found the same pattern. Starting did not require feeling ready. It required moving long enough for readiness to catch up.

## Takeaway

Notice when you are waiting to feel fresh before you begin, and ask whether rest is actually restoring you or protecting you from the first uncomfortable step. Start sore once — physically or mentally — and let motion show you the difference between damage and resistance.
