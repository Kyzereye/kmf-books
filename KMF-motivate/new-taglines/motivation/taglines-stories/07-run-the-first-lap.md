# Run The First Lap

*Run The First Lap*

You don't need to finish today — you need to begin today. Run the first lap and let the rest of the race argue with momentum, not fear.

## Category

**Motivation** — The finish line feels impossibly far when you have not moved; *Run The First Lap* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by shrinking the obligation to a single beginning instead of the whole distance.


## Theme

**Start Moving** — Beginning today without needing to finish today is the start move — the first lap is enough to break the stall.
## The idea

The mind refuses to start when it has already calculated how long the work will take and decided you are too tired, too behind, or too unprepared for all of it. You stare at the full race and negotiate with yourself until the day is gone. When you commit only to the first lap — one session, one mile, one pass through the material — you remove the argument about whether you can finish. Beginning creates its own logic. Once you are moving, fear has to chase you instead of blocking the door.

## Story
<!-- story shape: Before / after -->

On Tuesday at 6:15 in the morning, the track was empty and the training plan on your phone said five miles, but you had not run in three weeks. You laced your shoes while calculating the full distance, the October race, every lap you had skipped, and your legs felt heavy before you took a step because the whole race sat between you and the starting line while the negotiation in your head had already decided you were too behind to begin properly today.

Twelve minutes later you had run one lap around the block and stopped on purpose, not because you had failed but because one lap was the deal. You were winded but upright, walking home with your keys in your hand, and the half marathon had not moved closer in any magical sense. You were simply a person who had run today instead of a person who was planning to run someday, and the fear that had guarded the full distance never had to approve the first lap.

## Takeaway

When the whole task feels too large to touch, name the smallest honest beginning and do only that. Let tomorrow's distance worry about itself after you have proved you can start.
