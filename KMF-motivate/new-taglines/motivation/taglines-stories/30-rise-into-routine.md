# Rise Into Routine

*Rise Into Routine*

Willpower is expensive; routine is cheap. Rise into the same first three moves each day until starting feels like brushing your teeth — automatic, not argued.

## Category

**Motivation** — Arguing with yourself every morning burns the energy you need for the work; *Rise Into Routine* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by making the first moves automatic so starting no longer requires a daily negotiation.

## Theme

**Daily Discipline** — This entry fits here because it replaces expensive willpower with a fixed sequence repeated until the start becomes habit, not debate.

## The idea

Every morning you spend deciding whether to begin is a morning you spend paying the same tax twice — once on the argument and once on the work itself. Willpower is a limited account, and routine is the way to stop withdrawing from it before the real task even starts. The challenge is believing you need to feel fresh and motivated each day when what you actually need is a sequence so familiar your body runs it without asking permission. When the same first three moves repeat daily, starting stops feeling like a character test and starts feeling like brushing your teeth — automatic, slightly boring, and reliably done.

## Story

Tom used to lie in bed running a debate team in his head. Gym or not. Write or not. He could spend twenty minutes deciding and still end up doing nothing, exhausted from the argument before the first rep.

His sister, a nurse who worked rotating shifts, told him she did not decide whether to go to work. She got up, made coffee, put on her scrubs. The decision was in the sequence, not in the moment.

Tom wrote three moves on a card: water, shoes, door. Every morning, same order, no variations for the first week. Water first — a full glass before coffee. Shoes second — workout shoes by the bed, not searched for. Door third — outside or to the desk, depending on the day, but through the door before email.

The first days felt robotic. By day ten he noticed the debate had quieted. His feet were in the shoes before his mind had assembled a good excuse. He was not more motivated. He was more automatic.

A month later a friend asked how he had finally become consistent. Tom said he stopped trying to win the morning and started repeating three moves until winning was irrelevant.

## Takeaway

If starting still feels like a fresh battle every day, the problem may not be motivation but missing routine. Pick three small moves in a fixed order and repeat them until they run without negotiation. Save your willpower for the work itself, not for getting to the work.
