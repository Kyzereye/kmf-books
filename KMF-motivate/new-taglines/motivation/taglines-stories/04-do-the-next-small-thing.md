# Do The Next Small Thing

*Do The Next Small Thing*

When the whole project feels too big to touch, shrink it until your hands can reach it — one email, one page, one rep. Motion starts when the step is small enough to do.

## Category

**Motivation** — Overwhelm freezes you at the size of the whole; *Do The Next Small Thing* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by making the first action small enough that starting is no longer a debate.


## Theme

**Start Moving** — Shrinking the step until your hands can reach it is how motion breaks the paralysis of a project that feels too big to touch.
## The idea

The mind refuses to begin when the task is framed as everything at once. You are not failing because you lack ambition; you are stalled because the entry point is invisible. When you shrink the work to the next physical action — open the file, write one paragraph, make one call — you give yourself something you can control right now. Small starts are not small lives. They are how large lives actually get built.

## Story
<!-- story shape: Chronicle -->

The garage renovation lived in his head as one impossible object until he stopped asking how to finish it and started asking what could happen on an ordinary day. The first week he opened the door, swept one corner, and closed it again without drama; the permit stack on the counter did not get shorter, and that was fine. He measured one wall and wrote the number on the back of an envelope, watched one tutorial about drywall repair and stopped after one because one was the rule, and moved two boxes to the driveway on a day when his neighbor asked if he was moving and he said he was starting.

By the end of the second week he had cleared enough floor to stand with both feet inside. He did not frame a wall or solve the budget that week; he bought one tool he actually needed for the next step. Three weeks after that one section was framed. The garage was not done, but it was no longer a single impossible object in his head. It had become a sequence of small things he did on ordinary days, and the project began when the whole renovation stopped being the only question.

## Takeaway

When you are stuck, the problem is often the size of the frame, not the size of your ability. Find the next action so small it feels almost silly, do it on an ordinary day, and let the sequence of small things become the project itself.
