# One Brave Minute

*One Brave Minute*

You don't need an hour of courage — you need one minute of it. Give the work sixty seconds of your real attention and see what follows.

## Category

**Motivation** — Waiting to feel brave enough for a long session keeps you frozen; *One Brave Minute* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by asking for a single minute of real effort instead of an impossible reserve of courage.


## Theme

**Push Through** — Sixty seconds of real attention pushes through the need for an hour of courage.
## The idea

The mind demands a heroic window before it will begin: a quiet hour, a clear head, the right mood. That window rarely arrives on schedule, so the work stays untouched while you wait to feel ready. Courage does not usually show up as a long stretch of fearlessness. It shows up as one minute where you stop negotiating and give the task your actual attention. Sixty seconds is small enough that dread cannot fully organize against it, and often long enough for momentum to notice you have arrived.

## Story

Marcus had been avoiding a conversation with his landlord about a leaking pipe for two weeks. He rehearsed the call in the shower, drafted emails he never sent, and told himself he would handle it when he had time to stay on hold for forty minutes. The leak was still there. So was the dread.

Thursday afternoon he set a timer for one minute. He told himself he only had to dial the number and state the problem. If he wanted to hang up when the timer buzzed, he could.

He pressed call. Someone answered. He described the leak in two sentences. The woman on the line said a maintenance ticket would be opened and someone would come within forty-eight hours. The timer went off while he was still on the phone. He stayed for another three minutes because he was already in the conversation.

Marcus did not become a person who loves difficult calls. He became a person who had learned that one minute of real action costs less than two weeks of imagined catastrophe. The repair happened on Saturday. The dread had been louder than the phone call ever was.

## Takeaway

When fear is sizing up the whole task, shrink the entry to one minute of honest effort. You are not committing to bravery for an hour — you are committing to begin, and beginning often outlasts the timer.
