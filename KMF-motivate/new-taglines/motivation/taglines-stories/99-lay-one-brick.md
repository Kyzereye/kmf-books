# Lay One Brick

*Lay One Brick*

Walls are not built in leaps. Lay one brick today — one unit of real work — and trust the wall to appear one row at a time.

## Category

**Motivation** — Trying to finish the whole project in one leap keeps you standing in front of an empty wall; *Lay One Brick* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by doing one unit of real work today and trusting the structure to grow row by row.

## Theme

**Trust the Long Road** — This entry belongs here because meaningful results are assembled incrementally, and the wall only appears when you stop waiting to build it all at once.

## The idea

Big outcomes invite big gestures. You want to write the whole chapter, clear the whole inbox, transform the whole room — and when the whole thing feels impossible, you do nothing. Walls are not built in leaps. They are built one brick at a time: one unit of real work, placed today, whether or not you can see the finished structure from where you stand. The challenge is trusting that a single brick matters when the wall is not visible yet. Lay one today. The row appears after enough bricks, not before the first one.

## Story

Tom bought a fixer-upper with a backyard wall that had partially collapsed. He walked the property with a contractor who talked about footings, mortar, and a timeline measured in weekends, not afternoons. Tom went home and searched for weekend warriors who had finished entire walls in a single heroic day.

He found inspiration. He did not find a wall he could build alone before dark.

The next Saturday he bought twelve bricks and mixed a small batch of mortar. His neighbor watched from the driveway and asked how long the project would take. Tom said he did not know. He laid one brick, checked the level, and laid another.

He stopped after an hour with a row that would not impress anyone. It was level. It was real.

Every Saturday after that he laid what he could before lunch. By autumn the wall was not finished, but it was unmistakably underway — one row higher each week because he had stopped waiting to build the whole thing in a leap he could never actually take.

## Takeaway

When the full project paralyzes you, shrink the unit of work to one brick — one paragraph, one call, one task that counts on its own. Trust the wall to appear one row at a time, and place today's brick whether or not you can see the top from here.
