# Done Beats Perfect

*Done Beats Perfect*

A finished rough draft moves you forward; a perfect plan that never ships keeps you standing still. Stop polishing and start delivering — done is the only version that counts.

## Category

**Motivation** — Perfectionism delays the start; *Done Beats Perfect* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by making delivery — even rough — the action that breaks the stall.


## Theme

**Start Moving** — Shipping the rough version instead of endless polish is the move from standing still to delivering something real.
## The idea

The challenge is not a lack of talent but the quiet belief that your work must be flawless before anyone sees it. You keep refining the outline, the draft, the plan, while the real cost accumulates in the form of stalled momentum and a shrinking window of opportunity. When you finally choose delivery over endless polish, something shifts: the rough version enters the world, collects feedback, and teaches you what perfection never could. Done is not the enemy of good; it is the only version that actually moves you forward.

## Story
<!-- story shape: Juxtaposition -->

The same week, two people finished the same kind of work with the same hours and the same worries about quality, and only the definition of done separated them. Elena spent Monday through Thursday re-recording a welcome video because the lighting was almost right and the pause before the second bullet still felt wrong, and by Friday her outline had three new sections while the launch date on the calendar stayed blank. The course lived in a folder of improvements, polished in private and invisible to anyone who might have used it.

Across town, Daniel posted a workshop on Thursday night with a camera that shook, a worksheet full of typos, and a price lower than planned because the slides were not quite finished. By Sunday he had ten paying students and a short list of fixes pulled from real questions in the comments. Elena treated perfection as the price of entry; Daniel treated delivery as the entry itself and let the rough version teach what polishing alone never could.

## Takeaway

Notice where perfection has become a hiding place rather than a standard. The work you release will always feel slightly unfinished to you, and that feeling is not a signal to wait. It is a signal that you are close enough to learn in public. Ship the version you have, then let the next one be shaped by contact with the world instead of contact with your fear.
