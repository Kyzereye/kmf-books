# Put Hands On Work

*Put Hands On Work*

Thinking about the work is not the work — put your hands on it, literally if you can, and let touch break the trance of delay.

## Category

**Motivation** — Mental rehearsal substitutes for action until the task feels both urgent and impossible; *Put Hands On Work* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by moving from abstraction to physical contact with the material in front of you.


## Theme

**Start Moving** — Touching the work breaks the trance of delay — literally putting hands on it is the start move.
## The idea

The mind can loop on a task for hours without ever touching it. You plan, worry, optimize, and call that engagement while the actual objects — the document, the instrument, the tools, the ingredients — sit untouched. Physical contact changes the chemistry. Hands on work interrupts the trance of delay because the body reports something the imagination cannot: this is real, this is here, this responds when you move. You do not need inspiration to pick up the pen, open the file, or lay out the parts. You need contact first and clarity second.

## Story

<!-- story shape: Object story -->

The guitar had leaned in the corner for months while he thought about practice, watching finger-position videos at lunch and comparing picks and tuners and whether he was too old to start, and the instrument stayed quiet, wood and strings and a small nick on the third fret where someone's ring had caught it once.

One evening he stopped mid-thought and picked it up. The neck was cool against his palm, a string buzzed when he pressed too lightly, and when he adjusted and pressed again the note held imperfect and present and answering back. His shoulders dropped without permission while the loop in his head lost its grip. This was not the version of playing he had imagined from the couch; it was contact, pressure on wire and vibration through wood, a problem his hands could feel instead of rehearse. He put the guitar down after twenty minutes without sounding good, and he sounded started.

## Takeaway

When you notice yourself circling a task in thought, find the most direct physical entry point — the neck of the instrument, the clay, the dough — and touch the work within the next few minutes. Let sensation pull you out of rehearsal and into the version of the task that can actually change.
