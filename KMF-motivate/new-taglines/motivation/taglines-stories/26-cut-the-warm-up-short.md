# Cut The Warm Up Short

*Cut The Warm Up Short*

Warm-up can become avoidance with better branding — cut it short and get to the rep that actually counts.

## Category

**Motivation** — Endless preparation mimics progress while delaying the real effort; *Cut The Warm Up Short* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by limiting the runway before the work that actually moves the needle.


## Theme

**Daily Discipline** — Cutting warm-up before it becomes branded avoidance is discipline that gets you to the rep that actually counts.
## The idea

Stretching, organizing, reviewing notes, and "getting in the zone" all have legitimate uses — until they expand to fill the time you meant to spend performing. The challenge is telling the difference between readiness and disguise. When you cut the warm-up short, you keep the rituals that help and retire the ones that have become a waiting room. The rep that counts is rarely the fifteenth minute of preparation. It is the first minute of the thing you came to do.

## Story

Omar trained for a half marathon but could not break his habit of long pre-run routines. He scrolled playlists, adjusted his shoes twice, checked the weather app, and walked an extra block to "find his rhythm." By the time he started running, his window was half gone and his motivation had cooled.

His coach asked what counted as training. Omar said the miles. The coach asked what he was doing for the first twenty minutes. Omar did not answer.

The next week he set a rule: five minutes to lace up and step out the door. No playlist hunt. No extra loop. If the run felt bad, he could quit after one mile, but he had to start within five minutes of deciding to go.

The first few sessions felt abrupt. Then something shifted. He was logging more miles because he was spending less time performing readiness. The warm-up had been comfortable and invisible — avoidance with better branding. Cutting it short did not make running harder. It made starting honest.

## Takeaway

Audit what you call preparation and ask whether it is serving the work or replacing it. Set a short boundary on the warm-up and move to the rep that actually counts before comfort talks you out of it.
