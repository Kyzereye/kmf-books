# Tell Someone Today

*Tell Someone Today*

Private starts are easy to abandon. Tell someone today what you will begin and let the weight of your word do what comfort cannot.

## Category

**Motivation** — Private goals dissolve without witnesses; *Tell Someone Today* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by making a spoken promise to another person the lever that turns intention into something you cannot quietly drop.

## Theme

**Commit & Own It** — Saying it aloud to someone else stakes your word on the start, turning a private wish into a commitment you can be held to.

## The idea

You can tell yourself you will begin on Monday for months without ever feeling the cost of breaking the promise. The mind treats private plans gently. They bend, slide, and disappear without shame because no one was counting on you. Telling someone today changes the physics. Your words leave your head and enter a relationship. The other person may not police you, but you now know they heard you — and that quiet knowledge makes quitting feel different. The start does not have to be grand. It has to be spoken before comfort talks you out of it again.

## Story

Priya had been saying she would start studying for the broker's exam since January. She bought the book. She highlighted the table of contents. She never opened chapter one on the nights she planned to, because no one asked whether she had.

At lunch her sister mentioned a coworker who passed on the second try. Priya felt the familiar twinge — the one that meant she was about to add "I'm going to do that too" to the conversation and then forget it by dessert.

This time she said it differently. "I'm starting this week. Tuesday and Thursday, one hour." Her sister nodded. "Text me when you sit down Tuesday."

Priya almost laughed it off. Instead she said okay.

Tuesday came with the usual fatigue and a long message thread that wanted her attention. She remembered the lunch. She did not feel ready. She opened the book, sent her sister a one-word text — "Starting" — and read for forty-three minutes.

Nothing magical happened. What changed was smaller and more durable: the start was no longer a private fantasy she could revise without consequence. It was a promise she had put in someone's ear, and keeping it felt easier than explaining why she had not.

## Takeaway

Notice the goal you have been nursing in private because privacy keeps failure invisible. Tell one person today what you will begin and when — not as a dramatic announcement, but as a simple line you will remember you said. You are not asking for supervision. You are giving your word a witness, and that weight is often enough to make the first session real.
