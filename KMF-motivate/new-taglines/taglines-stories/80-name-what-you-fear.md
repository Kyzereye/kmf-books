# Name What You Fear

*Name What You Fear*

Fear grows vague and enormous in the dark — name what you are actually afraid of and it shrinks to a problem you can start on.

## Category

**Motivation** — Unnamed fear feels too large to act against; *Name What You Fear* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by turning vague dread into a specific problem small enough to take the first step.

## The idea

Fear without a name becomes everything at once — failure, judgment, wasted time, proof that you were not ready. It fills the room because you cannot argue with a fog. When you name what you are actually afraid of, the fear loses its ability to pose as total danger. It becomes one concern, often smaller and more workable than the dread suggested. A named fear is something you can plan against, test, or take one step toward despite.

## Story

Leo had been putting off a conversation with his manager about moving to a different team. He told himself he was "stressed about work," which sounded responsible and gave him nothing to do about it. He worked late. He snapped at his partner. He drafted emails he never sent.

His sister asked him to finish the sentence: stressed about what, exactly? He said he was afraid the conversation would go badly. She asked what "badly" meant. He sat with that.

He was afraid his manager would say he was not performing well enough to transfer. He was afraid he would sound ungrateful. He was afraid he would stay another year in a role that was slowly draining him because he could not tolerate ten minutes of discomfort.

Once he said it out loud, the fear did not disappear. It changed shape. It was not everything. It was three specific outcomes, and only one of them was likely. He wrote two sentences for the opening of the meeting. He asked for time on the calendar. The conversation was awkward. His manager was open to the move. The thing he had carried for two months turned out to be a meeting, not a catastrophe.

## Takeaway

When dread is keeping you still, write down the exact outcome you are afraid of — not "failure," but the sentence you do not want to hear. Naming it turns fog into a problem, and problems are what you know how to start on.
