# Give It Five Minutes

*Give It Five Minutes*

Five minutes is too small to fail and too real to fake. Give the work five honest minutes and let starting prove it belongs to you.

## Category

**Motivation** — The full task feels too large to touch; *Give It Five Minutes* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by shrinking resistance to a window so short that showing up becomes harder to refuse than to do.

## The idea

Resistance loves scale. Tell yourself you need a two-hour block and the day can pass without a single minute touching the work. Five minutes breaks that spell because it is almost insultingly small — too brief to require perfect conditions, too concrete to hide behind mood. An honest five minutes is not a trick to fool yourself forever. It is a fair test: does this task belong to you today, or are you only performing the identity of someone who will start later? Often the timer does not stop at five. Sometimes it does. Either way, you have traded paralysis for evidence.

## Story

Jonah stared at a tax folder every evening for two weeks. He told himself he needed a clear table, a quiet mind, and a full afternoon. The folder stayed closed. The anxiety grew anyway.

His therapist suggested a rule so simple it felt silly: five minutes, timer visible, no phone. If he wanted to stop when the alarm sounded, he could.

The first night Jonah set the timer mostly to prove the idea would not work. He opened the folder, sorted one envelope, and found a form he had been dreading was already filled out. The alarm rang and he kept going without deciding to. Not because he had become a different person, but because starting had lowered the temperature of the task.

Other nights he stopped at five minutes and still counted the day as a win. The folder stopped feeling like a moral verdict. It became paperwork he could touch in small doses.

By the time he filed, the hardest part had not been the math. It had been the months of treating a five-minute opening as beneath him while the undone work took up far more space in his head than it ever took on the clock.

## Takeaway

When you are avoiding something large, set a five-minute timer and work with honest attention until it ends. Let that sliver be enough to break the trance, and let whatever happens next — stopping or continuing — come from contact with the task instead of from another night of negotiation.
