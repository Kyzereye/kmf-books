# Stop Rehearsing Disaster

*Stop Rehearsing Disaster*

Your mind runs the worst version on a loop — make the call, send the message, and let reality replace the fiction. Most of what you dread never shows up; the rest you handle when it is real.

## Category

**Motivation** — Imagined catastrophe blocks the first move; *Stop Rehearsing Disaster* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by trading mental rehearsal for one real attempt.

## The idea

The brain treats a vivid worry like a fact, and you delay as if the worst version already happened. You cannot control how the conversation, meeting, or reply will go, but you can control whether you have it at all. One real attempt replaces ten imaginary ones, and reality is almost always smaller than the story fear wrote for you.

## Story

You had been carrying a conversation in your head for eleven days. In every version you played, your boss interrupted, your voice shook, or you were dismissed before you finished the first sentence. You drafted emails you never sent. You walked past her office twice and told yourself tomorrow would feel easier.

Tomorrow felt the same. The rehearsal never ended because you never gave reality a turn.

On Thursday you stopped negotiating. You asked for ten minutes. You said the thing without the perfect opening line you had composed in the shower. She listened. She asked one clarifying question. She did not throw anything. The catastrophe you had performed in private for a week and a half did not walk into the room.

Walking back to your desk, the relief was not triumph. It was space. The weight you had been carrying was not the conversation. It was the endless dress rehearsal for a disaster that was not happening.

## Takeaway

When you notice yourself rehearsing the worst outcome, treat that as a signal to act, not to think harder. Ask whether the problem is happening now or only in your head. If it is only in your head, one real step is the start.
