# Put Your Name On It

*Put Your Name On It*

Anonymous effort is easy to quit. Put your name on it — send it, submit it, attach it — and start as someone who can be held to account.

## Category

**Motivation** — Hidden work lets you quit without consequence; *Put Your Name On It* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by attaching your identity to the first step so walking away costs more than staying anonymous.

## Theme

**Commit & Own It** — Sending work under your own name makes you accountable in a way draft folders and private tabs never can.

## The idea

You can spend weeks on something that never carries your name — a draft in a folder, a sample in a private repo, a proposal you rewrite but never send. Anonymous effort feels safe because failure stays invisible. It also stays easy to abandon, because there is no version of you on the line. Putting your name on it does not require perfection. It requires submission: the email with your signature, the form with your contact field, the file uploaded where someone could reply. You start as a person who can be held to account, and that identity shifts how seriously you treat the next hour of work.

## Story

Elena had rebuilt the same portfolio site six times in a local folder. Each version was better than the last. None of them existed anywhere a client could find her. She told herself she would launch when the case studies felt strong enough to represent her.

A recruiter messaged her on a Wednesday asking for a link. Elena almost sent a PDF export with no contact page, no name in the header — just work, detached from a person who could be reached or judged.

She stared at the draft and recognized the pattern. Anonymous effort had let her iterate forever because nothing was truly at stake.

That afternoon she deployed the imperfect site, added her name to the footer, and replied with the URL. Her hands were unsteady hitting send. Within two days she had a call scheduled with someone who found a case study rough around the edges but clear about what she could do.

The site was not finished. It was hers. Putting her name on it did not expose her to catastrophe. It exposed her to forward motion — replies, questions, the next revision that only exists after someone knows who you are.

## Takeaway

Look for the work you are polishing in private because anonymity makes quitting painless. Put your name on one piece of it today — send, submit, attach, publish — even if the version feels early. You are not claiming to be finished. You are claiming to be present, and presence under your own name is where accountability begins.
