# Start With What Hurts

*Start With What Hurts*

The sore spot is often the real priority — start with what hurts instead of what is easy and watch honesty accelerate you.

## Category

**Motivation** — Avoiding discomfort keeps the real work at the bottom of the list; *Start With What Hurts* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by beginning with the task you have been dodging because it is the one that actually matters.

## The idea

The easy item on your list is rarely the important one. It is the buffer — the reply that feels productive, the tidy task that lets you avoid the conversation, the repair, the application, the truth you do not want to face. The sore spot persists because you keep scheduling around it. When you start with what hurts, you stop spending the whole day earning the right to avoid it. Honesty accelerates you because the weight you have been carrying in the background finally moves to the front, where it can be handled.

## Story

Rachel's to-do list looked full every morning and empty by evening in the ways that counted. She answered emails, organized files, and prepped for meetings that did not require her best thinking. The item that actually kept her awake — calling her brother about their father's care — sat untouched at the bottom for three weeks.

She told herself she needed the right headspace. She needed a clear afternoon. She needed to not cry on the phone. What she needed, she finally admitted, was to stop pretending the call was one of many equal tasks.

Tuesday she made it first. Before coffee, before the inbox, before the small wins that made her feel responsible while the real obligation rotted. Her hands were unsteady. The conversation was painful and incomplete. They did not solve everything in forty minutes.

But the dread that had been humming under every other task went quiet. The rest of her day felt strangely spacious. Work she had been pushing through got done faster, not because she had more time, but because she was no longer dragging an unspoken weight behind every click.

Rachel did not learn that hard conversations are pleasant. She learned that starting with what hurts clears the fog that easy work uses to hide in.

## Takeaway

Look at what you keep scheduling after everything else and ask whether it is actually lower priority or simply harder to face. Start with what hurts once and notice how much energy returns when you are no longer negotiating with the thing that matters most.
