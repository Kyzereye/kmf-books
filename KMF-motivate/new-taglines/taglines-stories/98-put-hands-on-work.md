# Put Hands On Work

*Put Hands On Work*

Thinking about the work is not the work — put your hands on it, literally if you can, and let touch break the trance of delay.

## Category

**Motivation** — Mental rehearsal substitutes for action until the task feels both urgent and impossible; *Put Hands On Work* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by moving from abstraction to physical contact with the material in front of you.

## The idea

The mind can loop on a task for hours without ever touching it. You plan, worry, optimize, and call that engagement while the actual objects — the document, the instrument, the tools, the ingredients — sit untouched. Physical contact changes the chemistry. Hands on work interrupts the trance of delay because the body reports something the imagination cannot: this is real, this is here, this responds when you move. You do not need inspiration to pick up the pen, open the file, or lay out the parts. You need contact first and clarity second.

## Story

Victor had been thinking about rebuilding his back porch all summer. He watched videos, compared lumber prices, and argued with himself about whether he should hire someone instead. Every evening he sat on the couch running the project in his head while the old boards creaked under his coffee mug.

His neighbor walked over with a borrowed pry bar and set it on the steps. "You have thought about this enough," he said. "Pull one board."

Victor almost launched into another explanation of the plan. Instead he knelt, fit the bar under a warped plank, and felt the nail resist, then give. The sound was ugly and satisfying. His shoulders tightened. Sweat came. The porch was no longer a debate. It was splinters, weight, and a problem his hands could answer.

He did not finish that day. He did finish the job over several weekends, correcting mistakes as he met them instead of predicting them from the couch. The difference had not been a better plan. It had been the first moment his body joined the conversation his mind had been having alone.

## Takeaway

When you notice yourself circling a task in thought, find the most direct physical entry point and touch the work within the next few minutes. Let sensation — pressure, texture, resistance — pull you out of rehearsal and into the version of the task that can actually change.
