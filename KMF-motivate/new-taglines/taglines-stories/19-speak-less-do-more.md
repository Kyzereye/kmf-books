# Speak Less Do More

*Speak Less Do More*

Talk is cheap and everyone has plans — let your actions be so loud they drown out what you said you'd do. The world doesn't need another announcement; it needs your execution.

## Category

**Motivation** — Talking about plans mimics progress; *Speak Less Do More* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by replacing announcement with the first finished piece of work.

## The idea

Announcing a goal can feel like progress because the praise arrives early — before the hard, invisible work has even begun — and that early reward makes the actual labor easier to postpone. Talk is cheap because everyone has plans, but execution is rare because it costs time, discomfort, and repeated failure in private. When your actions grow loud enough, they stop needing a speech to be believed.

## Story

You had told the same story at three dinners in one month — the business you were starting, the book you were outlining, the fitness goal you were serious about this time. People nodded. Some looked impressed. A few asked thoughtful questions. Nothing on the list was further along than when you first mentioned it, but every retelling gave you a small hit of identity, as if saying it aloud counted toward doing it.

Talking felt productive because it came with attention. You left those meals energized, already planning the next announcement instead of the next hour of work. The calendar stayed full of intentions. The finished column stayed empty.

You decided to go quiet. No launch post, no dramatic declaration, no rehearsed update about how focused you were. You blocked time on your calendar and treated those blocks like appointments you could not miss. You finished small pieces without telling anyone. A month passed before a friend asked what had happened to the project.

You opened your laptop and showed one result instead of ten intentions. The respect landed differently — not because you had performed humility, but because the work existed before the speech. Your actions finally spoke at a volume your plans never could, and you noticed how much energy you had been spending on being seen as someone who tries instead of on actually trying.

## Takeaway

Announcing a plan can deliver a small hit of satisfaction that feels like progress before any real work has happened. That feeling is seductive because it is easier than the labor itself, and it can quietly drain the urgency you need to follow through. Let your finished work introduce you when it is ready, and notice how much changes when your reputation is built on evidence instead of intentions. The world does not need another speech about what you are going to do. It needs the thing you actually did.
