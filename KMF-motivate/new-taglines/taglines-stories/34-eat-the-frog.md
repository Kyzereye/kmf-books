# Eat The Frog

*Eat The Frog*

Do the worst thing on your list first — the one you've been avoiding, dreading, or pretending isn't there. Swallow it early and the rest of your day tastes better.

## Category

**Motivation** — Avoidance makes the avoided task loom larger; *Eat The Frog* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by swallowing the worst task early and freeing the hours after it.

## The idea

The task you dread most is also the one stealing energy from everything else on your list, because your mind keeps flinching from it even while your hands are busy with safer work. Do that thing first — before email, before coffee, before your brain fully constructs its defense system — and the rest of the day opens up in a way that rearranging your schedule never achieves.

## Story

Your to-do list has one item glowing in the back of your mind: call the insurance company about the claim that has been denied twice. You have scheduled it every morning for a week. By noon you are deep in email, laundry, a podcast — anything with less hold music and fewer transfers to departments that cannot help you.

The avoidance is costing you more than the call ever could. You check the clock during other tasks and feel the frog sitting there, untouched, getting heavier. You tell yourself you will feel more prepared after lunch. You never do.

You read about eating the frog and feel personally attacked. Fine. Tomorrow, before coffee, before your inbox, before your brain can assemble its usual list of reasons to wait, you dial.

Forty-three minutes of hold music, repetition, and explaining the same facts to three different people. Your jaw clenches. You stay on the line. They escalate the case. You get a reference number. It is not fully resolved, but it is moving, and that is more than a week of "I'll do it later" ever produced.

You hang up and realize it is only 8:15. The rest of the day feels different — not because the list got shorter, but because nothing on it is worse than what you already handled. Tasks that usually slide to tomorrow get done today because the dread is gone.

The frog was not poisonous. It was just first. And first turned out to be the whole trick.

## Takeaway

Dread compounds when you defer it — each hour you wait adds weight to a task that was never as large as the energy you spent avoiding it. Swallow the worst thing early and you spend the rest of your day working instead of flinching.
