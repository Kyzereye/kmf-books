# Demand More From You

*Demand More From You*

Nobody else is going to push you as hard as you can push yourself — demand a higher standard from the one person who's always watching. You already know what you're capable of when you stop settling.

## Category

**Mindset** — Settling becomes invisible over time; *Demand More From You* answers "How do I change the way I think about this?" by raising the standard you hold yourself to when no one else will push.

## The idea

Your boss, your friends, even your coach will eventually stop pushing once you seem comfortable — but you are always in the room when you choose the easier version. The gap between where you are and where you could be is almost never a talent problem. It is a standard problem, and the one person who can raise that standard without asking permission is you.

## Story

You have been coasting on a reputation built two years ago. The work still lands on time. The feedback is still polite. But you know the difference between what you submit and what you are capable of, and lately the distance between those two things has become embarrassing in private.

It shows up in small cuts you do not make — the slide you leave generic because rewriting it would take twenty minutes, the workout you shorten because nobody is counting your reps, the conversation you smooth over because conflict would ruin a comfortable afternoon. Each choice is reasonable. Together they are a pattern, and the pattern has a name: settling.

One evening you review a deliverable the way you would review someone else's — not hunting for flaws to feel superior, but asking honestly whether this represents your best. The answer is no. Not catastrophically, not in a way anyone else would call out, but no in the way that matters when you are the one who has to live with the answer.

You raise the bar without announcing it. You rewrite the section that was fine. You rehearse the pitch until the transitions feel natural instead of memorized. You add the analysis you would normally skip because it was not explicitly requested. Nobody asked for more. That is the point.

At the next review your manager does not gush. She assigns you the larger account and mentions, almost in passing, that the last few submissions felt different — tighter, more considered. You nod. You know what changed. It was not the job. It was the person doing it finally demanding what they had been quietly withholding.

## Takeaway

The ceiling on your growth is usually the standard you accept from yourself, not the one anyone else sets for you. When you stop sandbagging in private, the world does not need to push harder — it just finally gets to see what you were holding back.
