# Close The Planning Tab

*Close The Planning Tab*

The plan is not the project — close the planning tab, open the work, and let execution teach you what the outline could not.

## Category

**Motivation** — Excessive planning substitutes for starting; *Close The Planning Tab* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by leaving the outline behind and opening the actual work so execution can supply what planning only guesses at.


## Theme

**Start Moving** — Closing the plan and opening the work shifts from thinking-about to doing — execution as the start move.
## The idea

There is a moment when planning stops clarifying and starts substituting. The outline grows. The color-coded calendar looks impressive. The research tabs multiply. None of it is the project — it is the performance of preparation, comfortable because it cannot fail in the way real work can. The plan will not teach you what execution will. Close the planning tab, open the work, and let the first messy pass show you where the outline was wrong, thin, or missing the point entirely.

## Story

<!-- story shape: Juxtaposition -->

The same afternoon, two people faced the same kind of project with the same number of hours, and one kept organizing the start while the other let execution argue with the plan. In one office a planning spreadsheet grew by the hour with new rows for audience segments, color codes for phases not yet started, and a third research tab comparing tools that would not matter until something existed to test, and each addition felt like progress because the cells filled in neatly.

In the other office the planning tab was closed and a blank file was open instead, one page of plain copy with a signup field that worked well enough. The design was rough and the outline was nowhere on screen, but version zero went live before the color-coding was finished. The spreadsheet could always absorb one more row; the live page could not hide inside preparation because the work was already public, small and imperfect and real, and questions from the first signup would teach more than another hour of color-coding ever could.

## Takeaway

Notice when the outline has become more satisfying than the work it describes. Close the planning tab, open the file, the call, the room, the draft — whatever the real project actually is. Let execution teach you what the plan could not, and let the first imperfect pass be the teacher instead of another night of preparation.
