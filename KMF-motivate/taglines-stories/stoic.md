Stoicism is a practical philosophy designed to help you navigate life's challenges by developing resilience, emotional mastery, and inner peace. It focuses entirely on controlling what you can and letting go of what you cannot, guided by four core virtues. [1, 2, 3, 4]  
The 4 Cardinal Virtues 
Stoicism rests on a foundation of four ethical pillars, which serve as a compass for daily decision-making: 

• Wisdom: The ability to navigate complex situations in a logical, informed, and calm manner. 
• Courage: The resilience to stand up for your values, face fear, and endure hardship without compromising your morals. 
• Justice: Treating others with fairness and kindness, recognizing that what is bad for the "hive" is bad for the "bee". 
• Temperance: The practice of self-control, moderation, and discipline over your desires and impulses. [4, 5, 6, 7, 8]  

Key Stoic Practices 
To truly master the philosophy, Stoics focus on a few actionable daily exercises: 

• The Dichotomy of Control: You cannot control external events, the weather, or other people's actions; you can only control your own thoughts, actions, and reactions. 
• Amor Fati: A mindset of not just accepting your fate, but actively embracing and loving everything that happens to you as an opportunity to practice virtue. 
• Premeditatio Malorum: The premeditation of evils. By mentally visualizing worst-case scenarios (like losing your job or a possession) ahead of time, you remove the element of surprise and prepare yourself to handle adversity. 
• Embody, Don't Preach: As suggested by Epictetus, Stoicism is a practice of personal responsibility; your actions should speak louder than your words. [6, 9, 16]
