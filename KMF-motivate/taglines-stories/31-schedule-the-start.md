# Schedule The Start

*Schedule The Start*

Unscheduled intentions evaporate. Schedule the start like a meeting you would not cancel and show up because the time block already decided.

## Category

**Motivation** — Vague intentions dissolve into the day; *Schedule The Start* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by putting the beginning on the calendar so the time block makes the decision before resistance wakes up.

## Theme

**Daily Discipline** — This entry belongs here because it treats the scheduled block as the commitment — the same way you would show up for a meeting — rather than leaving the start to whatever mood the afternoon brings.

## The idea

"I'll work on it later" is not a plan. It is a placeholder that evaporates the moment something louder arrives — a meeting, a message, the slow collapse of the afternoon. The challenge is treating important work like it lives outside time, as if intention alone were enough to protect it. When you schedule the start the way you would schedule a meeting you would not casually cancel, the block on the calendar does the deciding. You show up not because you feel ready but because the time already belongs to the work, and that simple shift turns floating intention into something that actually happens.

## Story

Rachel had been meaning to rebuild her portfolio for months. It lived on a sticky note, a mental item, a thing she would get to when the week calmed down. The week never calmed down.

Every day filled itself. Client work, errands, the reasonable-sounding tasks that felt productive but left the portfolio untouched. She was busy and stuck at the same time, which was its own kind of exhaustion.

Her coach asked one question: when, exactly, would she start? Rachel said she did not know — when she had a free afternoon. Her coach said free afternoons do not exist. They get invented by people who schedule them.

Rachel blocked Tuesday and Thursday, two to three p.m., on her calendar. She labeled it like a client meeting: Portfolio — Start. The first Tuesday she wanted to reschedule when an email came in at one-fifty-five. She did not. She closed the tab and opened the folder.

The first session was messy. She organized files she should have organized weeks ago and moved one section from old to new. It was not a breakthrough. It was a start that had a time attached to it.

Six weeks later the portfolio was live. Not because she finally found motivation, but because she had sixteen scheduled hours that the rest of her life had learned to work around.

## Takeaway

Notice what you keep intending to do without putting on the calendar. If it matters and it keeps slipping, the missing piece is probably not effort but a time block with your name on it. Schedule the start like you would schedule something you respect, and let the appointment answer the question of whether today is the day.
