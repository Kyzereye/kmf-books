# Start Year One

*Start Year One*

You are not in year five yet. Start year one with the patience it deserves and stop punishing yourself for not being further along.

## Category

**Motivation** — Comparing your beginning to someone else's middle kills the start; *Start Year One* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by accepting where you are and giving the first year the patience it actually needs.

## Theme

**Trust the Long Road** — This entry belongs here because every skill has a year one, and treating the early stage like a failure for not being year five guarantees you never reach year five.

## The idea

You watch someone five years into the craft and measure your week one against their year five. The comparison makes starting feel like already losing. You are not behind. You are early — and early is supposed to look different from experienced. Start year one with the patience it deserves: fewer shortcuts, more repetition, more permission to be clumsy. Punishing yourself for not being further along does not accelerate growth. It just makes you quit a timeline you were never on yet.

## Story

Priya started a podcast after listening to one that had been running for six years. The host's timing was effortless. The guests were names she recognized. The production sounded like a studio, not a spare bedroom.

Priya recorded her first episode four times and deleted all four. She told herself she would launch when she sounded like someone who belonged in the feed.

Her brother asked a simpler question. "How many episodes have they done?" She looked it up. Over three hundred. She had done zero that she was willing to publish.

She decided to treat the next month as year one on purpose — not a failed preview of year five, but its own stage. She published episode one with a long pause in the middle and an intro she knew she would cringe at later. Episode five was smoother. Episode twenty she stopped comparing her audio to hosts who had years of reps she had not earned yet.

Year one was not an embarrassment. It was the only way to reach year two.

## Takeaway

When you catch yourself measuring day one against someone else's year five, name the timeline honestly. Start where you are, give year one the patience it requires, and stop treating early as a synonym for failure.
