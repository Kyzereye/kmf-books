# Speak Less Do More

*Speak Less Do More*

Talk is cheap and everyone has plans — let your actions be so loud they drown out what you said you'd do. The world doesn't need another announcement; it needs your execution.

## Category

**Motivation** — Talking about plans mimics progress; *Speak Less Do More* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by replacing announcement with the first finished piece of work.


## Theme

**Start Moving** — This entry fits here because execution replaces announcement — the start move is letting completed work speak louder than stated plans.
## The idea

Announcing a goal can feel like progress because the praise arrives early — before the hard, invisible work has even begun — and that early reward makes the actual labor easier to postpone. Talk is cheap because everyone has plans, but execution is rare because it costs time, discomfort, and repeated failure in private. When your actions grow loud enough, they stop needing a speech to be believed.

## Story
story shape: Dialogue - tee shirt biz

At dinner his friend asked how the project was going, and he said he had been focused lately without offering anything she could touch. When she asked whether he had finished the first section, he explained that he was still refining the structure before putting anything out there, and she listened the way people listen when they have heard the same update before. He talked about research still coming in and timing that was not quite right, and he left the restaurant with the same warm feeling he always got after talking about the work, as if saying it aloud counted toward doing it.

Two weeks later, same table, same question. This time he opened the laptop before he answered. One file, one finished piece, no speech about focus or timing. She read the first page, looked up, and said quietly that this existed. That was the whole update, and the respect landed differently because the work had arrived before the speech.

## Takeaway

Announcing a plan can deliver a small hit of satisfaction that feels like progress before any real work has happened. That feeling is seductive because it is easier than the labor itself, and it can quietly drain the urgency you need to follow through. When someone asks what you are building, let the finished piece answer before your mouth does. Notice how much changes when your reputation is built on evidence instead of intentions.
