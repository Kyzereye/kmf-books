# Decide Then Move

*Decide Then Move*

Indecision dresses itself as preparation. Decide, then move within the same hour — before the mind reopens the case.

## Category

**Motivation** — Endless weighing keeps you at the starting line; *Decide Then Move* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by closing the decision and acting in the same hour before the mind finds new reasons to delay.

## Theme

**Mind Over Noise** — This entry belongs here because it cuts through the mental clutter of fake preparation — the reopened case, the second-guessing — by pairing decision with immediate motion.

## The idea

Indecision wears the clothes of careful thinking. You research one more option, ask one more person, sleep on it one more night, and call it preparation when it is actually avoidance with a respectable outfit. The challenge is that the mind reopens every case you leave sitting on the table, and each reopening costs another hour of motion you never get back. When you decide and move within the same hour — before the second thoughts assemble — you break the loop. The result is not perfect certainty but forward progress, and forward progress is what indecision was quietly preventing.

## Story

James had been choosing a platform for his side project for three weeks. He had spreadsheets, comparison charts, and a folder of articles arguing every side. Every time he nearly picked one, a new thread appeared with a reason to wait.

His partner finally said, "You're not preparing. You're postponing." James wanted to argue. He could not, because the project still had no landing page, no email list, no proof that anyone wanted what he was building.

That afternoon he set a timer for twenty minutes. He would pick one platform, sign up, and publish a rough page before the hour ended. Not the best platform. Not the final design. One decision, one move, same hour.

At minute nineteen he clicked publish on a page that embarrassed him slightly. It loaded. It worked. Within two days three people signed up for updates — not because the page was polished, but because it existed.

The mind tried to reopen the case that evening. Maybe he should have picked the other option. Maybe the headline was wrong. James noticed the familiar drift and went back to the list of people who had actually signed up. Reality had entered the conversation. Preparation had not.

## Takeaway

When you notice yourself gathering one more data point before you begin, ask whether you are preparing or postponing. Decide, then move in the same hour while the decision still has weight. The mind will reopen the case if you give it time — give it motion instead.
