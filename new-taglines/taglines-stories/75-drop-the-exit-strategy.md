# Drop The Exit Strategy

*Drop The Exit Strategy*

A quiet backup plan keeps you half-committed. Drop the exit strategy for one hour and pour yourself into the start.

## Category

**Motivation** — A hidden escape route softens commitment and slows the first real push; *Drop The Exit Strategy* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by giving the beginning one hour of full attention without a half-open door behind you.

## The idea

A backup plan feels responsible, but it can also keep you from pouring yourself into what is in front of you. Part of your mind stays packed — ready to pivot, ready to quit, ready to explain why this was never the real try. That split attention makes every start feel provisional. Dropping the exit strategy for one hour is not reckless. It is a way to find out what happens when you stop keeping one foot on the easier path.

## Story

Priya had been "exploring" freelance design for four months while keeping her job search active, her LinkedIn open, and a spreadsheet of backup careers she had not deleted. She told herself this was smart. She was covering her bases.

She also had not finished a portfolio piece. Every time she sat down to work on it, part of her calculated how long until she could reasonably give up. The project stayed at eighty percent. Her applications stayed generic. She was busy, but she was never fully in.

A mentor asked what would change if she committed to one hour with no exit. No checking job boards. No mentally ranking other paths. Just the portfolio, one hour, as if this were the thing she was actually doing.

Priya set a timer. She dropped the tab with job listings. She wrote the case study intro she had been avoiding, dropped in the screenshots, and adjusted the layout until the page looked like something she would send. When the timer rang, she had a finished piece and a strange feeling she had not earned in months — the sense that she had been present for her own work.

She did not burn her backups that day. She learned what one hour of full commitment produced, and that was more than four months of half-in effort.

## Takeaway

You do not have to delete every backup forever. For the next hour, close the mental escape routes and work as if this start is the one that counts. Notice how much more you move when you are not already halfway out the door.
