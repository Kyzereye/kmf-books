# Burn The Backup Plan

*Burn The Backup Plan*

Half-exits breed half-starts. Burn the backup plan for one hour and pour yourself into the beginning like retreat is not an option.

## Category

**Motivation** — Keeping an exit open lets you start without fully starting; *Burn The Backup Plan* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by removing the polite retreat that turns effort into a rehearsal.

## Theme

**Commit & Own It** — Eliminating the backup plan for one focused hour forces full investment instead of the hedged half-start that retreat always invites.

## The idea

Half-exits breed half-starts. You begin the business while keeping the job search warm. You write the novel with a mental note that you can always go back to consulting. The backup plan feels prudent. It also lets you pour half your attention into the beginning because retreat remains an option. Burning the backup plan does not mean reckless leaps. For one hour it means behaving as if this start is the start — no parallel escape running in the background. Pour yourself into the opening like leaving is not available, and watch how much more of you shows up.

## Story

Terrence had been "transitioning" to freelance design for eight months. He updated his portfolio. He told clients he was available. He also refreshed job boards every few days, just in case, just to stay realistic.

His first real project landed on a Thursday — a rebranding job with a tight timeline. He opened the files, then checked LinkedIn during the research phase. When the work got hard, part of him comforted itself with the thought that a steady job might still be out there if this did not work.

Friday morning he caught the pattern. He was not failing. He was starting with one foot already turned toward the door.

He closed the job tabs, turned off notifications, and wrote on a sticky note: "One hour. No backup plan." He set a timer and worked as if this client was the only path on the table — not because it was true forever, but because half-presence was producing half-work.

The hour was ordinary. Mood swings, a font he hated, a call he needed to return. What changed was depth. Without the silent backup plan humming in the background, he stopped rehearsing escape and solved the problem in front of him.

When the timer ended he had a direction the client approved that afternoon. The freelance life was still uncertain. For one hour he had burned the exit, and the beginning finally felt like a beginning.

## Takeaway

Notice the backup plan you keep running while you say you are starting — the job search, the easier project, the version of you that could still walk away without loss. Burn it for one hour. Work as if retreat is not an option, and let full attention do what half-starts never finish. You can revisit strategy later. First, give the beginning an hour of you without an exit.
