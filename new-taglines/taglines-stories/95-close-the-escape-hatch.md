# Close The Escape Hatch

*Close The Escape Hatch*

You keep leaving yourself a way out — close the escape hatch for one commitment and watch seriousness replace fantasy.

## Category

**Motivation** — Soft commitments let you pretend you are trying while preserving an exit; *Close The Escape Hatch* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by removing the polite backup plan that keeps effort theoretical.

## The idea

It is easy to announce a goal while quietly preserving an exit: the half-hearted application, the training plan you can abandon without embarrassment, the launch date you refuse to put on the calendar. Escape hatches feel responsible. They let you tell yourself you are serious while protecting you from the discomfort of being all in. The problem is that partial commitment produces partial energy. When you close the hatch on one decision — a date, a deposit, a public promise — seriousness replaces fantasy because the cost of retreat becomes real. That pressure is not cruelty. It is the condition under which most people finally act like they mean it.

## Story

Lena had signed up for a half marathon three times and quietly dropped out each time before training began. She told friends she might run in the spring, or fall, or whenever life settled down. She bought shoes. She downloaded plans. She always kept a mental note that she could skip it if work got busy, which work always did.

Her friend Rosa entered the same race and paid the nonrefundable fee in front of her. "I'm not giving myself an out," Rosa said. "You shouldn't either."

Lena felt the old reflex: keep it flexible, stay realistic, protect your pride. Then she registered before she could negotiate with herself, chose a training start date, and told her team she would be unavailable Sunday mornings. The decision was small on paper. It changed the texture of the weeks that followed.

Rainy mornings still came. So did sore legs and a schedule that did not soften to accommodate her. Because there was no graceful exit left, she adapted instead of fantasizing about a better month to begin. On race day she crossed the finish line slower than she had hoped and faster than every version of herself that had stayed on the sidelines with an open hatch.

## Takeaway

Look for the polite exit you have built around the thing you say matters. Close it on one commitment — money, date, witness — and let the seriousness that follows do what vague intention never could. You do not need a perfect plan. You need a decision that costs something to walk away from.
