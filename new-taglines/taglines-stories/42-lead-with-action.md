# Lead With Action

*Lead With Action*

Don't wait for permission, consensus, or perfect conditions — move first and let others follow the example. Leaders aren't appointed; they're demonstrated.

## Category

**Motivation** — Waiting for permission or consensus delays the first move; *Lead With Action* answers "How do I get started when I don't feel like it?" by acting first and letting others respond to a real example.

## The idea

When everyone waits for permission, consensus, or perfect conditions, problems sit in group chats and meeting notes while nothing moves. Move first — pick up the trash, draft the plan, make the call — and let others follow the example your hands set. Leadership isn't conferred in a title ceremony; it's demonstrated by the person willing to start before the room agrees it's time.

## Story

The team has talked about fixing the onboarding doc for six weeks. Three people own pieces. Nobody owns the whole thing. Every standup includes a polite "we should really get to that," and every Friday the doc is still a patchwork of outdated screenshots and contradictory steps. You feel the familiar itch of waiting for someone senior to declare it a priority.

Tuesday afternoon you block two hours, open the folder, and start. You don't announce a task force. You don't ask who's responsible. You rewrite the first five sections, flag the gaps you can't fill, and drop a link in the channel with a simple note: "Started here — add what's missing." For an hour, crickets. Then a designer drops updated screenshots. Then an engineer fixes the API example that was wrong for months. By Thursday the doc is something new hires can actually use.

Your manager mentions it in a one-on-one — not as a promotion speech, just a fact. "That thing sat forever until someone just did it." You realize nobody was waiting for authority. They were waiting for proof that starting wasn't career suicide. You gave them that by not waiting to be chosen.

## Takeaway

The first mover doesn't win because they're the smartest person in the room — they win because they convert paralysis into a template others can copy. Waiting for consensus is often a polite way of avoiding the risk of being wrong alone. Once someone acts, the social cost of joining drops fast, and that's when leadership actually spreads.
